Teletician

A systematic implementation to support personal fruit-intake behaviors in the home-based cardiac rehabilitation context.

This e-health project was a collaborative effort with Maxima Medisch Centrum in the Netherlands, aimed at addressing the issue of MMC's lack of a digital platform that could effectively track individual patient's food intake and goals. This led to frustration for patients and an increased burden on healthcare providers. Furthermore, the existing website was not user-friendly, particularly for senior patients who required accessible and effective feedback to make informed decisions. Our goal was to create scalable solutions that improved the overall user experience and promoted sustainable health behaviors in the telehealth domain.

Throughout the project, I engaged with multiple stakeholders, including industry experts and users, to gather feedback and co-create MVP. The process involved iterative research, design, and development phases, including agile development of IoT ecosystem solutions with the development team over three sprints.

For the final outcomes, the feedback received from behavior change metrics and scale expressed increased confidence in behavior change by an average of 21% by the targer users. The resulting product was presented for its strategic potential to add value to the existing rehabilitation platform, demonstrating its design opportunity for real-world execution.

E-health Journey in the home context 

Background & Problem 

• The e-health project partnered with MMC in the Netherlands to solve their problem of having the senior patients remotely self-tracking their dietary goals.

• The aim was to create a scalable solution that could integrate into the medical client’s existing workflow and can improve the senior patient group’s UX and make patients easier follow their dietary goals in a sustainable way.

 

Project management roadmap  

As the only UX designer, I employed design thinking to identify problems with various stakeholders, domain experts, and users. Utilizing lean UX and agile methods, I collaborated with developers to create the working product for shippable increments.

 

How do the medical stakeholders perceive the existing platform in tracking patients’ (diet) goals? and How do they envision the future of telerehabilitation?

Discovering the problems of the industry client's existing platform and inquesting the future scenario

At the beginning of the project, to discover the problem more in-depth, I want to understand how healthcare providers perceived the existing platforms for tracking patient (dietary) goals and envision the future scenario. To get started, I conducted a card-sorting session to validate the assumptions by topics and in the hierarchy.

The hierarchy validated results show that the medical stakeholders recognized that easy to use is most important for senior patients’ needs and efficiently collecting accurate information is important for healthcare providers to reduce workload.

So that I wanted to ensure that the design solution was not only functional but also intuitive enough to trigger sustainable behaviors among senior adults.

 

How does the user perceive the self-tracking methods on dietary behaviors in terms of easy-to-use and sustainable behaviors?

Participatory design methods to engage target user groups

Since The telehealth project has a great focus on behavior change, I knew that understanding the user's context was crucial, especially for the senior user groups, so I decided to I prototype participatory activities to discover how the users perceive the self-tracking approach, and inquest the assumptions by perceived values through diary study methods with user groups.

Through this process, I was able to gain a deeper understanding of what users expected and how we could make it more accessible for them to track their dietary behaviors. By tracking behavior change criteria and collecting feedback, I organized two main personas, one for those living alone and another for those living with partners.

These personas served as a guide for our design decisions, ensuring that we were addressing the unique needs of each user group. Through this approach, we were able to create solutions that not only met the functional requirements but also resonated with users and motivated behavior change.

 

How might we use an engaging behavior-tracking approach to help the senior patient groups to achieve their goals in a more sustainable way? 

Streamling the common values based on the feedback

Following this, I wanted to streamline feedback from medical stakeholders and users. To achieve this, I created 5 "How Might We" statements, the main one is asking:

How might we use an engaging behavior-tracking approach to help the senior patient groups to achieve their goals in a more sustainable way in the remote home context?

Through co-reflecting sessions with stakeholders, we discussed user journeys, experiences, personas, and scenarios while ideating solutions and generating design assumptions. To ensure we were prioritizing the design assumptions that had a higher perceived value to end-users and a higher feasibility on the operation to industry stakeholders.

 

After prioritizing the design assumptions, I formulated a hypothesis: "I believe that the combination of a tangible and digital user interface can engage senior patient groups' dietary behaviors as a daily routine, reducing their efforts in self-tracking and leading to sustainable impact."

 

What do the design features mean to the end users and the industry clients?

Define design scope and value propositions

After the propositions, I discussed with the industry client the features that wanted to include and improve. Therefore, the focus was to not only enhance the UX but also create a more efficient and accurate data collection approach for healthcare providers. In agile development, I worked closely with both software and electrical engineers to plan and create a product backlog. Prioritizing tasks based on importance and estimated effort, we then started developing the tangible device.

During the second sprint, we focused on ensuring a smooth and stable user-system interaction. I collaborated with the development team daily to achieve this. After presenting the realized features to the industry client, we moved on to develop the efficient behavior-tracking feature, which was integrated with an internet server for timely data access by healthcare providers.

Once the timeline was complete, I conducted user testing with senior groups to gain contextual insights about engaging behaviors. The feedback received was positive about the satisfaction of using such a tangible device in connection to their dietary goals and relationship with their healthy lifestyle. While the opinions on long-term sustainability vary, mainly because long-term scenarios could require longer-term experimentation to track. In that sense, customized features could be added to the tangible device for the flexible usage scenario.

How to integrate the tangible device into the existing rehabilitation flow to help senior patients lower the threshold of entering telerehabilitation?

Evaluation on UX system in telerehabilitation context

After finalizing the features of the tangible device, I discussed with the business industry client how we could integrate the design into the existing rehabilitation flow to help senior patients enter the telerehabilitation system more easily.

To accomplish this, I utilized user journey maps and process mapping to visualize the touchpoints between patients and healthcare providers. Based on these touchpoints, I generated design assumptions that were later programmed into usability testing to explore how senior adults feel about being guided through the digital journey, planned behavior-making, feedback receiving, and integration with other rehabilitation domains.

After conducting user testing, we found that participants were comfortable using our system design to achieve their dietary goals. They found the navigation and digital installation journey easy to follow, and they were satisfied with the feedback and suggestions provided. The lifestyle dashboard features, nevertheless, show some hierarchy and visual improvement room to make it easier for seniors to access feedback.

However, we did identify one critical issue during the testing: participants were uncertain about how to use the system when multiple goal-tracking devices were combined. To address this, it could be made possible that users to focus on one critical goal at a time, which could be discussed with their healthcare provider during the rehabilitation journey. This solution would be particularly useful if more rehabilitation domains were merged in the future.

 

Evaluation-based UI structure aroused from the challenge topics

Usability testing on the criteria

For the usability testing on UI, I invited five participants who had previously engaged in the home-deployment iteration to continue evaluating their experiences using the digital UI. The aim of the study was to assess how users recognized the connection between user data visualization and digital guidance through the telerehabilitation journey. By gathering insights from the study, we were able to identify areas where the digital UI could be improved to better support users in their telerehabilitation journey.

The qualitative user feedback showed that all participants were comfortable using the design solutions to achieve their dietary goals.However, (the challenge 4) the lifestyle dashboard features show some hierarchy and visual improvement room to make it easier for seniors to access feedback

Challenge 1: To investigate how well users can select the specified telerehabilitation domain(s), individual goal(s), and recognizing the specified the sensing tools and services.

Challenge 2:To learn how the users would think of/take the automatic suggestions (database) based on personalized profile

Challenge 3:To understand how user can recognize and do the installation through digital guidance at home

Challenge 4:To explore how users can connect to the data shown on the dashboard for self-reflection, and the interactive ways with chatbot

Challenge 5:To explore how users would identify the integration with different telerehabilitation activities on the digital platform

Demonstrator  

Based on the feedback and insights from the final iteration results, I created a new fruit bowl by 3D printing it in translucent material, making it more aesthetically pleasing. The translucent/transparent material allows the interactive light to pass through from any angle.

Learning & Takeaway

This Master Thesis Project was built upon the current architecture of the telerehabilitation system structured by Maxima MC, integrating the touchpoints with the healthcare providers from a patient-centered perspective. The final results of the project were presented to the client and stakeholders for the potential of real-world executions. This project allowed me to acquire valuable learnings:

 

Project management skills to work with involved stakeholders.

Because the project involved many stakeholders (clients, domain experts, and users), I learned the importance of advance planning and efficient communication with different parties for scheduling sessions and time management skills. In this project, I created a roadmap to file all the decision-making points and the time spent.

 

Design communication is important when presenting the design concept to stakeholders.        

Furthermore, to be more flexible during the iterative process of working with stakeholders, making planning and sharing (discussion) results properly is critical in communication and project management. Preparing alternatives (plan B) to adapt/pivot to different situations. Whereas, it is always important for a designer you could advocate why the design decision is better than other choices. 

 

MVPs are the balance of the user's needs and technical constraints within the limited timeframe.

I spent weeks with developers in the organization to experiment/test the stability and the effectiveness of the prototype, from which I learned the importance of identified efficient MVPs to process the iterative development is built open prioritizing user requirements and balancing out the design increments with various technical constraints within a limited time frame.

 

Design thinking asks for stakeholders' continuous involvement.

The methods of workshops and post-co-reflecting sessions to criticize the design from different perspectives can bring the design concept to a more grounded and realistic level for execution not only building the confidence of the teammates but the stakeholders

 

Challenge when designing for the systematic approach (combined with tangible and digital UI) in complex scenarios:

The combined tangible and digital approach were proven effective and had strong potential for the target patient groups, where different levels of interaction were involved to motivate the user groups to follow the target diet goals. However, despite the entire telerehabilitation structure within the design concept being discussed with different domain experts, one of the challenges that should be further evaluated with user groups is the longer-term scenario when several telerehabilitation domains are simultaneously executed so that the way to interact and the information to be presented can be investigated and prioritized to avoid the overwhelming information. As a service, prioritizing the functionalities with more impact or less effort on the experience could add value on a personalized level.
